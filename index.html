Becoming an Interview Master For Your Podcasts

2017-03-31

Guest Speaker: Michael O’Neal, Host of The Solopreneur Hour Podcast

Michael O’ Neal was a web and print designer, creative director, and branding expert for over 15 years before he started podcasting. He started The Solopreneur Hour Podcast in August 2013, and the show has about 8 million downloads as of Feb, 2017. Follow Michael on Twitter at @solohour.

Notes Organized by Jocelyn, at CastBox

Many people are launching their own podcasts nowadays. But the passion does fade out when the number of subscribers and downloads freeze at some point. One of the very effective marketing ways to promote your podcast would be interviewing an influencer and have him/her make an appearance in your show as a guest. However, the traditional style of just having a back-and-forth Q&A wouldn’t attract lots of attentions, and it may even lead to a negative result if people start thinking that your show is just as boring as those loquacious lectures.

So, the question then comes:

How to become an interview master and how it can massively grow your podcast?

In a session hosted by the Social Media Marketing World, Michael O’Neal has shared with us his strategies with us. Below are the notes we organized to share with all podcasters who are currently struggling with their interview techniques.

First, you want to ask yourself: what is the goal of having a big influencer on your show? Defining your targeted outcome is always important before you go into any action. Define your goal. Ask yourself what’s the most important you want to get out of this interview.

Also remember, don’t just ask questions. It’s really not about the questions you ask; it’s about how your guests feel at the end after you interview them. Do they also feel like this conversation beneficial? Do they also think this would be an informational episode to promote to their audiences as well? Have them given out the most helpful experiences they could share? Would they want to do more interviews with you after? These are all questions you need to consider before you actually getting into the interview if you want a long-term relationship with your guests.

Some people may wonder, I have my targeted audiences and outcome, but where can I find the right person to interview with? I’m a beginner in the podcasting industry, and there seems to be no connection for me to get in touch with my ideal interviewing candidates. There are three places Michael suggested where you could land amazing guests:

  1. Events. You need to know what your podcast’s topics, as well as your episode will be about, to find the right event to attend, and start making connections there. Those events do not have to be podcasting-related, as long as they meet your needs. For example, someone is hosting a social media strategies-related podcast. Then what might be a great event to go and make connections with? The Social Media Marketing World! Guests and speakers at these events are often very friendly in talking to you, as that’s why they are there for. So step out and find an event that you think it’s worth attending.

  2. 3rd Party. There are lots of places that could help you find and make connections with the perfect guests. You should also reach out to your candidates through your current connections in your own network. Never estimate the power of connections! You can literally get to anyone in the world with connections through only 6 people.

  3. Other Shows. Yes. Try listening to more podcasts and discover similar hosts like you. Make each other the guest of your podcast and help each other to reach more subscribers and exposure.

Great! Now you’ve found the perfect guest. What are some prep work you must do before starting your interview?

  1. Do your homework and research about your guest. Don’t just look at their about pages as everything there would be too general and boring for your interview as everyone can easily find those information online. Make sure you pronounce your guest’s name correctly! That’s very important. Also, look up on their social media accounts. You can always find interesting topics to discuss with them after you browse their daily social media activities. It’s very important to always have your interviewee’s social media in front of you.

  2. Make sure everything is set up correctly for the interview! Are your devices ready to record? Have you prepared enough so your interview won’t be disrupted by external reasons? Gotta double check before you hit the road!

What’s the most important thing your guest care about for you to interview them? For most of them, it’s the promotion! According to Michael, 85% people who listen to podcasts don’t make to the end of the show. (Yep that’s sad to hear.) SO, DON’T WAIT to promote your guest at the very end! Instead, promote him/her throughout the show! Make sure the promotion doesn’t sound very off. They are indeed doing you a favor.

And always remember to Control the Interview! Do not let your guest take over at any time. Remember to always speak to narrative, and always think about “community” in your mind. You can always start with the question “tell me the story of …” and therefore lead up to a great story the guest may never talked to anyone before. A great question Michael suggests that everyone can ask is the question of “difference”. For instance, what’s the difference between the new you and the old you? Would you have done something differently? They are always great leading questions for guest interviews. Michael also shared with us his all-time favorite question to ask his guests at the beginning of interviews: what did your childhood smell like? As he said, the creatives would start answering while the data-driven people would not really understand the question. Therefore, you could then decide how would you run your interview base on the answers you get from that question.

Last but not least, you must get your guests to share your show to maximize the result. Do not be shy. Ask your guest straight if he or she is willing to share the show. Get their promises onsite, and email them later with a simple note saying “thank so much for promising to share the show.” Yep it’s tricky, but it always works! 80% of the people would actually be willing to share the show, so remember to give out a trackable CTR for episode or channel link, so you could calculate your results in the end.

And the very last, if you are a shy host, don’t be afraid to do a pre-scripted show!
